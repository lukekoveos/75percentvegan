## Wait, This Is Actually About Science

Here's the thing about the 75% approach: it started as a philosophy. Eat mostly plants. Don't be weird about it. Progress over perfection.

But then the science showed up. And it turns out the research agrees with the vibes.

A growing body of peer-reviewed evidence — from *The Lancet* to *BMC Medicine* to Cambridge and Harvard — keeps landing on the same number: **reducing animal product consumption by roughly 25–30% produces measurable, significant health benefits.** Not "might help." Not "could potentially." Measurable. Significant.

That's not a coincidence. That's the thesis.

This page is the overview. No moralizing. No "meat is murder." Just: here's what happens inside your body when you eat more plants. You can do whatever you want with that information.

## Your Heart (The Headline Numbers)

Let's start with the big one.

In July 2024, *The Lancet Planetary Health* published a microsimulation study modelling what would happen if the US adult population reduced processed meat consumption by just 30%.

The results:

- **92,500 fewer cardiovascular disease cases** over a decade
- **352,900 fewer type 2 diabetes cases**
- **53,300 fewer colorectal cancer cases**

And when they modelled the same 30% reduction for unprocessed red meat? An additional **382,400 fewer CVD cases** (Harvard Health, October 2024).

A separate meta-analysis in *BMC Medicine* (2023) found that substituting animal-based foods with plant-based alternatives was consistently associated with **lower risk of cardiovascular disease, type 2 diabetes, and all-cause mortality**.

These are not fringe studies. These are the journals your doctor reads.

## Your Gut (The Weird but Fascinating Bit)

Your gut contains roughly 38 trillion microorganisms. They outnumber your human cells. They have opinions about what you eat.

Research from Tim Spector and the ZOE project found that people who eat **30 or more different plants per week** have significantly more diverse gut bacteria than those eating fewer than 10. That diversity matters — it's linked to better immune function, better digestion, and better overall health.

Here's the kicker: animal products contain **zero dietary fiber**. And fiber is what feeds your good gut bacteria. According to a review in *PMC* (2021), **95% of Americans are fiber deficient** — largely because they're not eating enough fruits, vegetables, legumes, and whole grains.

The 75% approach naturally fixes this. More plants = more fiber = happier gut tenants.

## Your Brain (Nobody Talks About This)

This is where it gets weird. And interesting.

About 90% of your body's serotonin — the neurotransmitter associated with mood regulation — is produced in your gut. Not your brain. Your gut.

Research published in *Gut Microbiome* (Cambridge, 2024) found **positive associations between higher dietary fiber intake and better cognitive and affective processes**. Translation: more fiber, better mood, clearer thinking.

A 2021 study in *Microbiome* (Springer) went further: a fiber-deprived diet caused **cognitive impairment via hippocampal microglia-mediated synaptic loss**. That's science-speak for "not eating enough fiber literally damages the part of your brain responsible for memory."

Nobody puts that on the bacon packaging.

## Your Long-Term Odds

Longevity research keeps pointing in the same direction.

A 2025 review in the *Journal of Biomedical Science* identified fiber intake, regular exercise, and pre/probiotic supplementation as the key interventions for fostering vitality in later years — all mediated through the gut microbiome.

*PLOS One* (2024) found that vegetarian and vegan diets significantly improved metabolic profiles: **reduced total cholesterol, LDL cholesterol, fasting blood glucose, and HbA1c** (a marker of long-term blood sugar control).

You don't have to go fully plant-based to see benefits. But eating more plants, more often, keeps showing up as one of the most consistent signals in nutrition science.

## The 30% Effect: Why 75% Isn't Just Philosophy

Here's what's genuinely interesting: the 75% approach was designed as a practical philosophy — "eat plants most of the time, don't stress about the rest."

But the Lancet study modelled a **30% reduction**. That's almost exactly what 75% plant-based looks like in practice. The number wasn't reverse-engineered from the science. It just happens to line up.

When you eat 75% plant-based, you're landing in the range that peer-reviewed research associates with:

- Lower cardiovascular risk
- Better cholesterol profiles
- Reduced type 2 diabetes risk
- More diverse gut microbiome
- Better cognitive and mood outcomes
- Improved long-term metabolic health

That's not a sales pitch. That's a literature review.

Science is interesting. But it doesn't feed you at 7pm on a Tuesday.

And here's a bonus finding: cooking itself has measurable mental health benefits. A systematic review by Farmer, Touchton-Leonard & Ross (2018, *PMC*) found that cooking interventions improved **self-esteem, social interaction, reduced anxiety, and improved quality of life**. A separate study using the PERMA wellbeing model (*PMC*, 2021) found that cooking simultaneously hits five dimensions of psychological wellbeing: positive emotion, engagement, relationships, meaning, and accomplishment.

So when you spend a Sunday afternoon batch-cooking a [75% Chili](/recipes/75-percent-chili/) or a [Chickpea Curry](/recipes/chickpea-curry/) — that's not just meal prep. That's therapy with a side of fiber.
