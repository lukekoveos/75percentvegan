## The Gut-Brain Axis: Not a Metaphor

There's a nerve running from your gut to your brain called the vagus nerve. It's not decorative. It's a two-way communication highway carrying chemical signals that influence your mood, your stress levels, and your ability to think clearly.

Scientists call this the gut-brain axis, and it's one of the most active areas of neuroscience research right now. Not because it's trendy. Because the data keeps landing and it keeps being weird.

Your gut bacteria — all 38 trillion of them — are producing neurotransmitters, short-chain fatty acids, and immune signals that your brain actually responds to. You didn't hire them. You can't fire them. But what you feed them changes what they send upstairs.

## 90% of Serotonin Is Made in Your Gut (Seriously)

Here's the fact that stops people mid-conversation: roughly 90% of your body's serotonin — the neurotransmitter associated with mood regulation, sleep, and general "feeling okay" — is produced in your gastrointestinal tract, not your brain (Yano et al., *Cell*, 2015).

Your gut's enterochromaffin cells crank out serotonin based on what's happening in your digestive environment. The composition of your gut microbiome directly influences this process. Specific bacterial strains — particularly from the *Lactobacillus* and *Bifidobacterium* families — are involved in serotonin and GABA production.

This doesn't mean eating a salad is an antidepressant. But it does mean the stuff going on in your gut has a measurable relationship with the chemicals that regulate your emotional state.

That's not wellness talk. That's biochemistry.

## What Fiber Has to Do With Your Mood

Fiber is gut bacteria food. Full stop. When you eat dietary fiber, your gut bacteria ferment it into short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs) like butyrate, propionate, and acetate. These aren't obscure molecules — they're directly involved in reducing inflammation, maintaining the gut barrier, and signaling the brain via the vagus nerve.

A 2024 study published in *Gut Microbiome* (Cambridge University Press) found positive associations between higher dietary fiber intake and better cognitive and affective processes. People who ate more fiber reported better mood and showed improved performance on cognitive tests (Berding et al., *Gut Microbiome*, 2024).

Here's the uncomfortable part: 95% of Americans are fiber deficient (Quagliani & Felt-Gunderson, *American Journal of Lifestyle Medicine*, 2017). Animal products contain exactly zero dietary fiber. If most of your meals are built around meat and dairy, your gut bacteria are essentially running on fumes.

A fiber-deprived diet doesn't just make you constipated. A study in *Microbiome* (Springer, 2021) demonstrated that fiber deprivation caused cognitive impairment in mice through hippocampal microglia-mediated synaptic loss. In plain English: starve your gut bacteria and your brain starts losing connections.

## The Studies That Got Scientists Excited

This isn't one rogue paper. The evidence is stacking up across multiple research groups:

**The fiber-cognition link:** A systematic review published in PMC found that dietary fiber intake was associated with improved cognitive function and reduced risk of cognitive decline. The mechanism runs through the microbiota-gut-brain axis — fiber feeds bacteria, bacteria produce metabolites, metabolites influence brain function (Berding et al., *Nutritional Neuroscience*, 2021).

**Reshaping the microbiome works:** A 2024 study in *Nature* showed that dietary fibers could reshape the gut microbiome in ways that mitigated both cognitive and social impairments. Not "might." Did. In controlled conditions.

**The mental health connection:** A review in *Nutrients* (2021) concluded that "using healthy diets to positively modulate gut-brain communication holds possibilities for prevention and treatment of common mental disorders." That's peer-reviewed researchers saying food choices have therapeutic potential for anxiety and depression.

**The developmental angle:** Research published in *Springer* (2025) established that the microbiome gut-brain axis impacts brain development and that synbiotics — combinations of prebiotics and probiotics — can ameliorate neurological problems.

None of this means "eat kale, cure depression." Mental health is complex, and anyone telling you food is a replacement for professional treatment is selling something. But the evidence that what you eat meaningfully influences your mental state is getting harder to wave away.

## You Don't Need a PhD. You Need More Lentils.

The practical takeaway from all this research is surprisingly simple: eat more plants, eat more variety, and your gut bacteria will produce better signals.

Tim Spector's research at ZOE found that people eating 30+ different plants per week have significantly more diverse gut microbiomes than those eating fewer than 10. Diversity is the key — not volume, variety. Herbs count. Spices count. That turmeric in your curry is doing something.

Here's what this looks like in real meals:

**High-fiber, gut-friendly options:**
- Lentils (one cup = ~15g fiber, plus prebiotic starches)
- Chickpeas (hello, [Easy Chickpea Curry](/recipes/chickpea-curry/))
- Black beans (the [75% Chili](/recipes/75-percent-chili/) is basically a gut bacteria party)
- Oats, whole grains, nuts, seeds
- Any vegetable you actually enjoy eating

**Quick wins:**
- Add a handful of mixed seeds to your breakfast
- Throw an extra vegetable into whatever you're already cooking
- Swap white rice for brown or mixed grains once a week
- Eat the skin on your potatoes (fiber lives there)

You're not trying to overhaul your life. You're feeding the 38 trillion organisms that are already running a significant portion of your neurochemistry.

## What This Means for the 75% Approach

The gut-brain research makes a quietly powerful case for the 75% philosophy. You don't need to eliminate all animal products to improve your gut microbiome diversity. You need to *add more plants*.

That's the whole point. The [75% approach](/philosophy/) isn't about what you subtract. It's about what you gain when most of your meals are built around whole plant foods.

Your gut bacteria don't care about labels. They care about fiber, polyphenols, and variety. Give them that, and the signals they send your brain get measurably better.

Nobody's asking you to become a different person. Just eat more things that grew in soil.
