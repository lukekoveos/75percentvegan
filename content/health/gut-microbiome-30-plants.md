## Your Gut Has 38 Trillion Tenants (They Have Opinions About Lunch)

There are roughly 38 trillion microorganisms living in your gut right now. That's more than the number of human cells in your body. They're bacteria, fungi, viruses — a whole ecosystem doing its thing while you go about your day.

This ecosystem — your gut microbiome — affects your digestion, your immune system, your mood, and possibly your long-term health outcomes. It's not a metaphor. It's biology.

And these microorganisms have preferences. They like variety. Specifically, they like plant variety.

Research from Tim Spector and the ZOE project found that people who eat **30 or more different plants per week** have a significantly more diverse gut microbiome than people eating fewer than 10. And microbiome diversity is consistently linked to better health outcomes — stronger immune function, better digestion, and reduced inflammation.

The good news: 30 is a lot more achievable than it sounds.

## The 30-Plant Rule (And Why It's Weirdly Achievable)

When Spector first started talking about the 30-plant rule (covered in *The Guardian*, May 2022, and *Business Insider*, May 2024), people panicked. Thirty?! That's a lot of vegetables.

But "plants" doesn't just mean the stuff in the produce aisle. The definition is broader than you think — and that's what makes this doable.

The rule isn't about eating 30 servings. It's about eating 30 **different types** of plants across a whole week. Every distinct plant species counts as one point.

Most people are already halfway there without realizing it.

## What Counts as a "Plant"? (Good News: More Than You'd Think)

This is the part that changes everything. All of these count as individual plants:

**Fruits & Vegetables** — obviously. An apple, a banana, broccoli, carrots. Each one is a point.

**Herbs & Spices** — basil, cilantro, cumin, turmeric, black pepper, garlic, ginger. Every distinct herb or spice counts. A well-spiced curry can rack up 5–6 points on its own.

**Grains** — rice, oats, wheat, quinoa, barley, corn. Each grain is a separate plant.

**Legumes** — black beans, chickpeas, lentils, peanuts, kidney beans, edamame. Each one counts.

**Nuts & Seeds** — almonds, walnuts, flaxseed, chia, sunflower seeds, sesame.

**Tea & Coffee** — yes, really. Different teas (green, black, chamomile) come from different plants. Coffee counts.

**Fermented plants** — sauerkraut (cabbage), kimchi, miso (soybeans). Still plants.

Let's do a quick count on a single day:

- **Breakfast:** Oats (1) with banana (2), blueberries (3), flaxseed (4), and a coffee (5). That's 5 plants before 9am.
- **Lunch:** Hummus (chickpeas = 6) wrap with spinach (7), tomato (8), cucumber (9), and a squeeze of lemon (10).
- **Dinner:** [Chickpea Curry](/recipes/chickpea-curry/) with onion (11), garlic (12), ginger (13), cumin (14), turmeric (15), coconut milk (16), and rice (17).

Seventeen plants in one day. Without trying particularly hard. Do that across a full week with some variety, and 30 is not just possible — it's almost inevitable.

## Why Diversity > Volume

Here's the counterintuitive part: eating a mountain of broccoli every day is less useful for your microbiome than eating moderate amounts of 30 different plants.

Your gut bacteria are specialists. Different species thrive on different types of fiber, different polyphenols, different prebiotics. When you eat the same three vegetables on rotation, you're feeding the same few bacterial colonies and starving the rest.

Diversity of input creates diversity of microbes. And diverse microbiomes are more resilient — they bounce back faster from illness, antibiotics, and stress.

ZOE's research found that people eating 30+ plants per week had gut microbiomes that looked fundamentally different from those eating fewer than 10 — not just slightly better, but measurably more robust in terms of beneficial bacteria populations.

Think of it like this: you wouldn't hire a company where every employee had the same skill set. Your gut works the same way.

## What Happens When You're Fiber-Deficient (It's Worse Than You Think)

A review published in *PMC* (2021) found that **95% of Americans are fiber deficient**. Not slightly low. Deficient.

The recommended daily intake is about 25g for women and 38g for men. Most people get around 15g. That's a massive gap.

Why does this matter? Because fiber is what feeds your gut microbiome. It's the primary fuel source for beneficial bacteria. Without enough of it, those bacterial populations decline, gut barrier function weakens, and inflammation increases.

And here's the fact that nobody mentions on the packaging: **animal products contain zero dietary fiber.** None. Not meat, not cheese, not eggs, not fish. Zero grams.

If your diet is heavy on animal products and light on plants, you're almost certainly not getting enough fiber. Not because animal products are "bad" — but because they don't contain the nutrient your gut microbiome needs most.

A 2021 study in *Microbiome* (Springer) took this further: a fiber-deprived diet caused **cognitive impairment in animal models through hippocampal microglia-mediated synaptic loss**. Low fiber wasn't just a gut problem — it was a brain problem.

The *Journal of Biomedical Science* (2025) identified fiber intake as one of the key interventions for gut-mediated longevity and vitality in later years.

The fix is not complicated. It's just... eat more plants.

## The 75% Connection: More Plants Doesn't Mean No Meat

This is where the 30-plant rule and the 75% approach fit together perfectly.

The 30-plant rule doesn't say "don't eat meat." Tim Spector eats meat. The ZOE research isn't an anti-meat campaign. It's a pro-diversity campaign.

You can eat a steak and still hit 30 plants in a week. You can have chicken for dinner and still get your fiber. The question isn't "meat or no meat" — it's "are you eating enough different plants alongside whatever else you're eating?"

The 75% approach naturally solves this. When most of your meals are plant-forward, you're automatically getting more variety, more fiber, and more of the prebiotics and polyphenols that your gut bacteria actually want.

It's not about restriction. It's about addition.

## Simple Hacks to Hit 30 Plants This Week

No need to overhaul your pantry. Just add variety where you can.

**The spice rack shortcut.** A single curry uses cumin, turmeric, coriander, ginger, garlic, and chili. That's 6 plants from the spice rack alone. The [Chickpea Curry](/recipes/chickpea-curry/) and [Quick Lentil Soup](/recipes/quick-lentil-soup/) are both plant-point machines.

**The mixed bag trick.** Buy mixed salad leaves, frozen mixed vegetables, trail mix, mixed bean cans. Each distinct item in the mix counts separately.

**The "one new thing" rule.** Each time you shop, add one plant you don't usually buy. A new herb. A different grain. A weird squash. Over a month, that's 4–5 new plants in rotation.

**The seed sprinkle.** Keep a jar of mixed seeds (flax, chia, sunflower, pumpkin) and sprinkle them on everything — oats, salads, stir-fries. Four points for zero effort.

**The diversity jar.** This is Spector's own concept (from his *Guardian* interview): keep a jar in the kitchen and add a bean or seed for every new plant you eat that week. Visual tracking. Weirdly motivating.

**Batch cook with variety.** The [75% Chili](/recipes/75-percent-chili/) uses beans, tomatoes, peppers, onion, garlic, cumin, chili powder, and corn. That's 8+ plants in a single batch that feeds you all week.
