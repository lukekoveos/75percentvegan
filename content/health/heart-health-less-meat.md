## The Numbers That Started This Conversation

In July 2024, *The Lancet Planetary Health* published a study that put hard numbers on something nutritionists had been saying for years.

They modelled what would happen if the US adult population reduced processed meat consumption by 30%. Not eliminated. Reduced.

The results, projected over a single decade:

- **92,500 fewer cardiovascular disease cases**
- **352,900 fewer type 2 diabetes cases**
- **53,300 fewer colorectal cancer cases**

When they added a 30% reduction in unprocessed red meat to the model, the cardiovascular number jumped by another **382,400 cases prevented** (Harvard Health, October 2024).

That's not a rounding error. That's a mid-sized city's worth of heart attacks, strokes, and diagnoses — avoided by a shift that most people wouldn't even notice at dinner.

## What Actually Happens to Your Cholesterol

Your body needs cholesterol. It makes most of what it needs on its own. The problem is when LDL ("bad") cholesterol builds up in your arteries, forming plaques that narrow blood vessels and increase the risk of heart attack and stroke.

A 2023 meta-analysis (*PubMed*, PMID 37226630) looked at the pooled evidence from multiple studies and found that vegetarian and vegan diets were associated with **significantly reduced total cholesterol, LDL cholesterol, and apolipoprotein B** — a protein that carries LDL particles through your bloodstream.

A separate study in *PLOS One* (2024) confirmed the pattern: plant-heavy diets significantly improved metabolic markers including **total cholesterol, LDL, fasting blood glucose, and HbA1c**.

You don't have to go fully plant-based to see changes. The data consistently shows that **more plants and less animal protein = better lipid profiles**. It's dose-dependent, not all-or-nothing.

## The Processed Meat Problem (Different from Red Meat)

This is worth understanding, because the science treats these differently.

**Processed meat** — bacon, sausages, hot dogs, deli meat, anything cured or smoked — carries a stronger and more consistent association with cardiovascular disease than unprocessed red meat.

The American Heart Association published a review (*CIRCULATIONAHA.118.035225*) showing that higher intake of processed and red meat was linked to increased risk of both type 2 diabetes and cardiovascular disease. But the processed meat signal was louder.

A 2025 paper in *JACC CardioOncology* identified chronic inflammation as the shared mechanistic pathway — processed meat drives inflammatory markers that contribute to both CVD and cancer through overlapping biological mechanisms.

Why does this matter? Because the easiest, highest-impact change isn't "stop eating steak." It's "eat less bacon and sausage." That's a much smaller ask with a disproportionately large payoff.

## How Much of a Difference Does 30% Actually Make?

Let's make it concrete.

Say you currently eat processed meat 10 times a week (breakfast sausage, deli sandwich, bacon on a burger, pepperoni pizza — it adds up faster than you think).

A 30% reduction means going from 10 to 7 servings. Three fewer servings per week. That's it.

According to the Lancet model, if everyone in the US made that single change, the country would see nearly **half a million fewer cases of serious chronic disease** over ten years.

Scale that to the individual: you're meaningfully reducing your personal risk of heart disease and diabetes by swapping three meals a week. Not overhauling your entire diet. Three meals.

That's the 75% approach in a nutshell. Small shift, real numbers.

## What "Heart Healthy" Actually Looks Like in Practice

This isn't about eating salad for every meal. Nobody wants that. Nobody sustains that.

Here's what "30% less processed meat" looks like in a normal week:

**Swap breakfast sausage for:** Scrambled eggs with spinach, a [Breakfast Burrito](/recipes/breakfast-burrito/) loaded with beans and peppers, or just toast with peanut butter.

**Swap the deli meat sandwich for:** Hummus wrap, leftover [Chickpea Curry](/recipes/chickpea-curry/) in a thermos, or a bean and cheese quesadilla.

**Swap the pepperoni pizza for:** A veggie pizza. Still pizza. Still good. Or make [75% Tacos](/recipes/75-percent-tacos/) instead — all the flavor, fraction of the processed meat.

**Keep the steak on Saturday.** Seriously. The science is about averages over time, not individual meals. If you're eating plants for most of the week, a steak dinner isn't the thing that moves your risk profile.

The [75% Bolognaise](/recipes/75-percent-bolognaise/) is the proof of concept here — it uses 25% meat, 75% lentils and vegetables, and tastes like bolognaise. Not like compromise. Not like sacrifice. Like food.

## The One-Sentence Summary

A 30% reduction in processed meat — roughly three fewer servings per week — is associated with significantly lower risk of cardiovascular disease, type 2 diabetes, and colorectal cancer, with the strongest evidence coming from the largest and most recent studies in the field.

Your heart doesn't care about your philosophical stance on veganism. It cares about what's flowing through your arteries. The data suggests more plants and less processed meat gives it a better working environment.

That's not a lecture. It's just math.
