## Your Playlist Is Doing More Than You Think

You probably already listen to music while cooking. Or commuting. Or doing the dishes. You do it because it feels good.

Turns out, "feels good" has a measurable biological basis — and it goes beyond mood. Music actively changes what's happening in your body: your stress hormones drop, your immune markers shift, and your nervous system calms down in ways that researchers can track in real time.

This isn't wellness fluff. This is peer-reviewed, replicated science. And it has a surprisingly neat connection to the whole [75% Vegan philosophy](/philosophy/) — but we'll get to that.

## Cortisol: The Stress Hormone Music Actually Lowers

Cortisol is your body's primary stress hormone. It's useful in short bursts — the fight-or-flight stuff. But chronically elevated cortisol is linked to high blood pressure, weight gain, weakened immune function, and poor sleep.

Here's where music gets interesting.

A comprehensive review published in *Frontiers in Psychology* (2023) found that music listening reduces stress through two pathways simultaneously: it calms the **hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis** (the system that produces cortisol) and it dials down **sympathetic nervous system activity** (the "fight or flight" branch of your autonomic nervous system). Most studies in the review showed a measurable decrease in cortisol levels during and after music listening (doi:10.3389/fpsyg.2023.1108402).

An earlier study by Khalfa et al. (2003), referenced in the same review, found that after a stressor, people who listened to music saw their cortisol levels **drop back to baseline faster** than people who sat in silence.

And a 2013 study published in *PLOS One* (Scheider et al., PMC3734071) confirmed that music measurably suppresses stress-related cortisol increases — not just subjectively ("I feel calmer") but objectively, in blood samples.

So next time someone tells you to "just relax," you can tell them you're already on it. You pressed play.

## The Immune System Connection

This one is genuinely surprising.

Research published in PMC (PMC3110826) found that patients who listened to instrumental music during surgical procedures showed measurable changes in cortisol, ACTH (adrenocorticotropic hormone), and — here's the kicker — **IgA levels**. IgA (immunoglobulin A) is an antibody that plays a key role in mucosal immunity. Higher IgA = better front-line immune defense.

The HeartMath Institute's research library documents a broader pattern: positive emotional states — including those triggered by music — are associated with improved immune system markers. The mechanism appears to involve the autonomic nervous system's influence on immune regulation.

This doesn't mean listening to the [75% Vegan playlist](/music/) will prevent you from catching a cold. But it does mean that the positive emotional states music creates aren't just "nice to have" — they're linked to measurable changes in how your immune system performs.

## Music While Cooking: Double the Effect?

Here's where the 75% site gets to feel a little smug.

We already know that [cooking has measurable mental health benefits](/health/cooking-therapy-mental-health/). A systematic review by Farmer, Touchton-Leonard & Ross (2018, PMC5862744) found that cooking interventions improve self-esteem, reduce anxiety, and increase psychological wellbeing. The PERMA model research (PMC8071848) shows cooking simultaneously hits five dimensions of wellbeing: Positive Emotion, Engagement, Relationships, Meaning, and Accomplishment.

And now we know music independently reduces cortisol and supports immune function.

So: **cooking with music on is stacking two evidence-based stress interventions at the same time.** You're not just making dinner. You're running a DIY wellness protocol.

## What Kind of Music Works Best?

Here's the honest answer: **whatever you actually like.**

The research shows some patterns — slower tempo music (60-80 BPM) tends to produce the strongest relaxation effects, and instrumental music sometimes outperforms lyrical tracks in clinical settings. But the most consistent finding across studies is that **personal preference matters more than genre.**

A review in PMC (PMC9480195, 2022) noted that the stress-reduction effects of music are mediated by both physiological mechanisms and personal emotional response. Translation: if death metal genuinely makes you happy, it's probably reducing your cortisol too.

The 75% playlist covers a lot of ground — over 40 versions of the same song in different languages. Some are upbeat, some are mellow. Find the version that clicks for you. The science says your body will respond to the one that resonates, not the one that's "supposed to" be relaxing.
