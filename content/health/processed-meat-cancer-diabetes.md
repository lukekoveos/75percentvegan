## Let's Start With the Numbers (Then We'll Talk)

In July 2024, a microsimulation study published in *The Lancet Planetary Health* modelled what would happen if the US adult population reduced processed meat consumption by 30%.

The numbers over a 10-year period:

- **352,900 fewer cases of type 2 diabetes**
- **92,500 fewer cases of cardiovascular disease**
- **53,300 fewer cases of colorectal cancer**

That's from a 30% reduction. Not elimination. Not going vegan. Just eating roughly a third less bacon, sausage, deli meat, and hot dogs than the current average.

Those are big numbers. They deserve attention. They also deserve context, which is what this article is for.

## What "Group 1 Carcinogen" Actually Means (It's Not What You Think)

In 2015, the International Agency for Research on Cancer (IARC) — part of the World Health Organization — classified processed meat as a Group 1 carcinogen. Headlines went predictably bonkers. "BACON AS DANGEROUS AS CIGARETTES" was a common take.

That take was wrong. Here's why.

The IARC classification system grades the **strength of evidence**, not the **degree of risk**. Group 1 means "sufficient evidence that this agent causes cancer in humans." It says nothing about how much cancer, or how likely you are to get it.

Tobacco is Group 1. Processed meat is Group 1. Alcohol is Group 1. Sunlight is Group 1. These are not equally dangerous. They're equally well-studied.

To put it in actual numbers: the Global Burden of Disease Project estimates that diets high in processed meat contribute to about **34,000 cancer deaths worldwide per year**. Tobacco causes approximately **1 million cancer deaths per year**. Alcohol causes about **600,000**.

Processed meat is a Group 1 carcinogen because the evidence is clear and consistent that it increases colorectal cancer risk. But the magnitude of that risk is nowhere near tobacco, and anyone who tells you otherwise is misreading the classification system.

**What the IARC actually said:** Each 50-gram portion of processed meat eaten daily increases colorectal cancer risk by about 18%. That's 50 grams — roughly two slices of bacon or one hot dog — every single day.

That 18% is a *relative* risk increase. If your baseline lifetime risk of colorectal cancer is about 5%, an 18% relative increase brings it to about 5.9%. That's a real increase. It's not a death sentence.

## The Diabetes Connection: 352,900 Cases in One Decade

The processed meat story isn't only about cancer. The Lancet 2024 data on type 2 diabetes is arguably more striking.

352,900 fewer diabetes cases from a 30% reduction in processed meat across the US population. That's not a rounding error. Type 2 diabetes affects roughly 37 million Americans and costs the healthcare system over $400 billion annually. Any intervention that meaningfully reduces incidence is significant.

The mechanism is reasonably well understood. Processed meats contain:

- **Nitrates and nitrites** (preservatives that can damage pancreatic beta cells)
- **High sodium** (associated with insulin resistance)
- **Advanced glycation end products (AGEs)** formed during high-heat processing (promote chronic inflammation)
- **Heme iron** in concentrated amounts (linked to oxidative stress and beta cell dysfunction)

A 2025 review in *JACC CardioOncology* identified chronic inflammation as the shared mechanistic pathway linking processed meat consumption with both cardiovascular disease and cancer. It's not a coincidence that the same foods keep appearing in multiple disease categories — the underlying biology is connected.

The American Heart Association's position, published in *Circulation* (2020), is straightforward: higher intake of processed and red meat is associated with higher risk of type 2 diabetes and cardiovascular disease.

## The Colorectal Cancer Reality

Colorectal cancer is the third most common cancer worldwide. The evidence linking it to processed meat consumption is among the most consistent findings in nutritional epidemiology.

Here's what the data actually shows:

- The Lancet 2024 study: a 30% reduction in processed meat = **53,300 fewer colorectal cancer cases** over 10 years in the US
- The IARC dose-response relationship: ~18% increase in relative risk per 50g/day of processed meat
- Multiple prospective cohort studies have replicated this association across different populations

To be clear about what this does and doesn't mean:

**It means:** Regular, high consumption of processed meat is a consistent risk factor for colorectal cancer. Reducing intake reduces risk. The effect is dose-dependent — less is better, proportionally.

**It doesn't mean:** Eating a hot dog at a barbecue will give you cancer. Occasional consumption is not what the epidemiology is measuring. The risk applies to habitual, daily or near-daily intake patterns.

**It also doesn't mean:** Unprocessed red meat carries the same risk. The IARC classified unprocessed red meat as Group 2A — "probably carcinogenic" — which is a meaningfully different category. The evidence for unprocessed red meat is less consistent, and the distinction between a grass-fed steak and a package of hot dogs is real from a biochemistry standpoint.

## What This Doesn't Mean

This is the section that matters most for the 75% approach.

**It doesn't mean you should never eat processed meat again.** The studies are measuring population-level risk from habitual consumption patterns. Eating salami on holiday in Italy isn't the same as eating bacon every morning for 30 years.

**It doesn't mean all meat is equally problematic.** Processed meat (bacon, sausage, deli meat, hot dogs — anything preserved by smoking, curing, salting, or adding chemical preservatives) has a significantly stronger evidence base for health harms than unprocessed red meat. And unprocessed red meat has a stronger evidence base than poultry or fish, which show neutral or even positive associations in some studies.

**It doesn't mean the data is perfect.** Nutritional epidemiology is inherently messy. People who eat a lot of processed meat may also smoke more, exercise less, or have other confounding lifestyle factors. Researchers control for these variables, but no observational study is airtight.

**It doesn't mean fear should drive your choices.** The numbers are what they are. A 30% reduction in processed meat — basically the [75% approach](/philosophy/) applied to one food category — produces measurable population-level health benefits. That's a reason to make a change, not a reason to panic about what you ate last Tuesday.

## The 75% Maths Applied Here

Here's where the Lancet data gets interesting for anyone already thinking about the 75% approach.

The study modelled a **30% reduction** in processed meat. Not 100%. Not 50%. Thirty percent.

The 75% philosophy is built on the idea that eating plant-based 75% of the time — and eating whatever you want the other 25% — produces meaningful impact without requiring perfection. A 30% reduction in processed meat fits comfortably within that framework.

What does a 30% reduction look like in practice?

- If you eat bacon 5 mornings a week, make it 3 (or fewer)
- If you have a deli meat sandwich every lunch, swap 2 of those for hummus or bean-based options
- If sausages are a weekly dinner, try [75% Meatballs](/recipes/75-percent-meatballs/) instead — same comfort, less processed content
- If hot dogs are the default barbecue option, mix in some [75% Tacos](/recipes/75-percent-tacos/) with bean filling

You don't have to eliminate anything. You just shift the ratio.

The [Math Behind 75%](/blog/the-math-behind-75-percent/) article lays out the environmental case. This is the health case. Different data, same conclusion: you don't need to be perfect. You need to be slightly different, most of the time.

352,900 fewer diabetes cases. 92,500 fewer heart disease cases. 53,300 fewer cancer cases. From a 30% change.

The data doesn't require your opinion. It's just sitting there, being true.
